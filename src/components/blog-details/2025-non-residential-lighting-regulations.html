2025 Non-Residential Lighting Regulations: What Every Business Needs to Know

Starting in 2025, new federal and state efficiency standards will reshape how commercial buildings use lighting. Learn what’s changing, who’s affected, and how upgrading to LED now can keep your business compliant while lowering costs.
January 21, 2025
Dave Kobuszewski

The U.S. Department of Energy and several state energy commissions are introducing updated non-residential lighting regulations in 2025. These standards are designed to improve efficiency, reduce carbon emissions, and lower long-term operating costs for commercial properties.

Who Is Affected?

The regulations apply to most non-residential facilities including office buildings, retail stores, warehouses, manufacturing plants, and educational institutions. Any business that operates lighting systems installed before 2015 is likely to be out of compliance without upgrades.

Key Changes Coming in 2025

  • Minimum Efficiency Standards: Fluorescent and incandescent fixtures will no longer meet legal requirements.
  • Lighting Power Density (LPD) Limits: Stricter watt-per-square-foot allowances for new construction and major renovations.
  • Automatic Controls: Occupancy sensors, daylight harvesting, and advanced controls will be required in many spaces.
  • Phase-Out of Older Bulbs: Several high-wattage lamps will be banned from sale and installation.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Businesses that fail to comply may face higher inspection costs, increased utility rates, or penalties tied to building code enforcement. In some jurisdictions, non-compliance could even delay occupancy permits for new projects.

How LED Upgrades Help

Converting to LED lighting not only ensures compliance but also provides immediate financial benefits. LEDs use 50–80% less energy, last years longer, and qualify for generous Duke Energy rebates that cover up to 75% of project costs.

Next Steps for Businesses

If your facility still relies on fluorescent or metal halide fixtures, now is the time to act. Conduct an energy audit, explore rebate opportunities, and create a phased upgrade plan to spread costs over time while securing incentives.
